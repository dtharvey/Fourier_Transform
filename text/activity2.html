A single peak in the frequency domain corresponds to a single damped oscillating wave in the time domain. If there are two peaks in the frequency domain, then the time domain signal should show evidence of both peaks. You can use the controls on the right to explore the relationship between two Gaussian peaks in the frequency domain and their corresponding contributions to the time domain signal. By default, both peaks are included in the frequency domain spectrum and in the time domain spectrum; you may, however, use the radio buttons to isolate the individual peaks and their individual time domain signals.

Exercise 1. The default inputs create two peaks with identical widths and areas. The first peak is centered at a frequency of \(40\) and the second peak at a frequency of \(60\). Examine the time domain spectrum and explain how its pattern is similar to and different from that for a system with a single peak with a frequency of \(40\) or \(60\).

Exercise 2. Make a prediction for what you will see in the frequency domain and in the time domain if a system has two peaks with the same position (\(50\)), the same width (\(10\)), and the same area (\(10\)). Adjust the sliders to create this system and then evaluate your prediction.

Exercise 3. Adjust the sliders so peak 1 has a position of \(40\), a width of 5, and an area of \(10\), and so peak 2 has a position of \(60\), a width of 5, and an area of \(10\). Predict how the time domain spectrum will change if you adjust both peaks to a width of \(10\) and then evaluate your prediction.

Exercise 4. Adjust the sliders for peak 1 to a position of \(40\), a width of 5, and an area of \(10\), and adjust the sliders for peak 2 to a position of \(50\), a width of 5, and an area of \(10\). Working in steps of +1 or +2 along the frequency axis, adjust the position of peak 1 from \(40\) to \(60\) and describe how the time domain spectrum changes.

Exercise 5. What do you think the time domain spectrum will look like if there are more than two peaks in the frequency domain?
